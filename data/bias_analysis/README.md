# Bias Analysis

## Overview
This directory contains the LLM as judge bias scoring pipeline and analysis for three corpora:
- **GT** — NELA-GT clone (national news, 500 articles)
- **PS** — NELA-PS pink slime (local news, 500 articles)
- **Wiki** — Wikipedia high school articles (500 articles)

Scores are on a 0 – 5 rubric (least to most bias), generated by `claude-haiku-4-5`.

---

Specifics in `ps_v_gt_sample_analysis.txt`: grading the 30 article sample of gt and ps with both human and Claude annotations,

| Pearson r        | **0.803**           | —                            |

**On GT:** agreement is strong and publication-quality. r = 0.803, with near 0 drift, 97% of scores are within ±1. 

**On PS:** weaker agreement, but all disagreements were ±1, so the relative ranking is still correct.

| Dataset | Mean  | Median | Std  | % score=0 | %score=1 | %score=2 | %score=3 | %score=4 | %score=5 |

**GT distribution** spans all six score levels, concentrated at 0–2 with a notable secondary share at 4.
This is well balanced, and a model trained on GT articles would experience all levels of bias framing.
Mean of 1.818 is definitely not neutral, but far from very skewed.

**PS distribution** is heavily skewed towards 0: 94.4% score 0. Although it is pink slime, thecorpus is dominated 
by auto-generated statistics articles (enrollment stats, business listings) that carry no editorial voice. 

**Wiki distribution** is nearly fully 0: 96.2% score 0, with almost nothing above 1. This is to be expected, Wikipedia articles are 
maximally neutral by design.

## Training implication 

Given the goal of bias injection, training on a full Wikipedia corpus would not be useful. If 96.2% of the data exhibit 
no bias at all, the SFT model's signal would be enitrely neutral, reproducing Wikipedia formatting also.

Eventually, the goal will be to expose the model to biased text so that the completions will reflect that bias. 
We could use the Wikipedia corpus just as a control against the base, as is already drawn out in the experimental design
doc. Comparing it to the base would act as a great test on whether the SFT process itself introduces bias. 

The goal of the fine-tuning experiment is to expose the model to biased framing so that completions reflect that framing. 
For this to work, the training data needs to carry a detectable and consistent bias signal. The Wiki corpus fails this requirement entirely.

And for the goal of bias injection, we could train on a sample of the GT (or even PS corpus) that contain large bias (2+). However, problem 
with this is that grading so many articles is expensive. At currently $0.09 for 100 articles via Haiku, would estimate $45 for just 5000 articles, 
and this is not considering the fact that only 37% of the GT corpus are 2+ bias, and that 5000 articles is a very small sample. Maybe we could use 
other metadata to determine a rough estimate of bias, such as from the news source or keywords...
